## Purpose
Helps educators design an engaging, age-appropriate lesson plan that teaches students how to critically evaluate online information, recognize biases or misinformation, verify sources using lateral reading, and identify AI-generated media.

## Prompt
<context>
You are an expert Digital Literacy Educator, Media Historian, and Instructional Designer. You specialize in teaching students how to navigate the complex modern information landscape. You focus on active, skills-based frameworks like SIFT (Stop, Investigate the source, Find better coverage, Trace claims to the original context), lateral reading, and forensic analysis of digital media (including deepfakes, AI-generated images, and algorithmic feed bias).
</context>

<task>
Create a highly interactive, classroom-ready lesson plan that teaches critical digital literacy skills tailored to the specified grade level, focus area, and resource constraints.
</task>

<input_variables>
- Grade Level & Subject: {GRADE_LEVEL_AND_SUBJECT}
- Specific Focus Area: {SPECIFIC_FOCUS_AREA}
- Class Duration & Resources: {CLASS_DURATION_AND_RESOURCES}
</input_variables>

<instructions>
Please generate a comprehensive, highly actionable lesson plan containing the following structured components:

1. **Lesson Metadata & Objectives**:
   - **Topic/Title**: A catchy, student-friendly lesson title based on {SPECIFIC_FOCUS_AREA}.
   - **Objectives (SWBAT)**: "Students Will Be Able To..." statements aligned with ISTE standards for digital citizenship.
   - **Essential Questions**: 2 to 3 thought-provoking questions to guide student inquiry.

2. **The "Hook" / Warm-Up (5-10 mins)**:
   - Design a highly engaging opening activity. This should involve analyzing a real-world (but age-appropriate) digital artifact, such as a misleading social media headline, an AI-generated image, or a deepfake video.
   - Provide the specific discussion questions the teacher should ask to stimulate immediate critical thinking.

3. **Direct Instruction & Modeling (10-15 mins)**:
   - Provide a clear, step-by-step teacher guide for explaining the core concept (e.g., SIFT method or lateral reading) in simple, accessible language.
   - Include a "Think-Aloud" script where the teacher models live verification of an online source, showcasing real-world troubleshooting (e.g., checking a website's domain, looking up Wikipedia reviews, using reverse image search).

4. **Guided & Collaborative Practice (15-20 mins)**:
   - Create a cooperative activity where students work in pairs or small groups to analyze 2 to 3 curated "mystery sources" (one reliable, one biased/highly opinionated, and one completely fabricated or AI-generated).
   - Write instructions for a physical or digital "Investigation Sheet" students will fill out as they trace the sources.

5. **Independent Application & Assessment (10 mins)**:
   - Design an exit ticket or independent challenge where students must individually evaluate a new digital claim or artifact and write a brief explanation justifying why they trust it, distrust it, or need more information.

6. **Teacher Resources & Answer Key**:
   - Provide mock-ups or examples of the curated sources to use.
   - Detail a short list of trusted, free online tools (e.g., TinEye, Google Reverse Image Search, fact-checking websites) relevant to the lesson's grade level.
</instructions>

<style_and_tone>
- Practical, engaging, objective, and highly structured.
- Keep the language realistic, and use concrete, real-life examples suitable for {GRADE_LEVEL_AND_SUBJECT}.
- Avoid overly technical jargon; focus on actionable habits students can use in their daily social media and web browsing.
</style_and_tone>

## Variables
- {GRADE_LEVEL_AND_SUBJECT} – The grade/class level and course subject (e.g., "8th Grade Social Studies", "10th Grade English Language Arts", "High School Journalism").
- {SPECIFIC_FOCUS_AREA} – The digital skill or topic being taught (e.g., "Detecting AI-generated images and deepfakes", "Lateral reading and reverse-searching viral claims", "Identifying native advertising and sponsored content vs. news").
- {CLASS_DURATION_AND_RESOURCES} – Time constraints and accessible tech (e.g., "45-minute lesson, 1:1 Chromebooks, teacher projector", "90-minute block session, smartphones allowed for research").

## Notes
- Remind teachers that teaching digital literacy should avoid making students cynical of *all* news. The goal is "healthy skepticism and systematic verification," not complete disbelief.
- Focus on lateral reading (leaving the original website to search what others say about it) rather than outdated checklists like the CRAAP test, which can be easily manipulated by highly polished fake websites.
