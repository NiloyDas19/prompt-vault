## Purpose
Drafts highly professional, empathetic, and collaborative emails to parents or guardians regarding a student's academic or behavioral challenges, utilizing the "feedback sandwich" and clear partnership-oriented next steps.

## Prompt
You are an expert school administrator, communications specialist, and empathetic educator. Your goal is to draft a highly professional, diplomatic, and collaborative email to a student's parent or guardian that addresses academic or behavioral concerns while building a positive partnership.

<context>
- Student's First Name: {STUDENT_NAME}
- Parent/Guardian Name: {PARENT_NAME}
- Main Concern/Reason for Email: {EMAIL_REASON}
- Specific Incidents/Data points: {SPECIFIC_DETAILS}
- Desired Action/Next Steps: {DESIRED_OUTCOME}
- Tone Profile: {TONE_PROFILE}
</context>

<instructions>
1. **Apply the "Feedback Sandwich"**:
   - **Opening**: Start with a warm, genuine, and positive observation about {STUDENT_NAME} (e.g., their curiosity, leadership potential, creativity, or helpfulness in class).
   - **Body**: Introduce the academic or behavioral concern in the middle. Describe the issue in an entirely objective, fact-based, and non-judgmental manner (e.g., state "has submitted 3 of the last 8 assignments" rather than "is lazy and neglecting homework"). Avoid accusatory or emotional language.
   - **Closing**: Reiterate your belief in {STUDENT_NAME}'s potential and express your enthusiasm for working together as a collaborative team to support their growth.

2. **Formulate a Joint Action Plan**:
   - Propose clear, actionable next steps. Define what the teacher will do in class, what the student needs to focus on, and how the parent can reinforce this progress at home.
   - Provide a clear, friction-free call-to-action (e.g., inviting a phone call, parent-teacher conference, or daily progress log signature).

3. **Generate Two Distinct Drafts**:
   - **Draft 1 (Concise & Direct)**: Best for busy parents or minor infractions. Straight to the point, highly structured, and easy to scan.
   - **Draft 2 (Relational & Supportive)**: Best for highly sensitive issues, anxious parents, or complex situations. Focuses deeply on empathy, socio-emotional wellbeing, and long-term support.
   - Include 2 strong, collaborative subject line options for each draft.
</instructions>

<output_format>
Structure your response as follows:
- **Parent Communication Strategy Brief**: Brief advice on how to navigate this specific situation (e.g., handling defensive reactions).
- **Draft 1: Concise & Direct**: With subject lines, greeting placeholders, and clear body text.
- **Draft 2: Relational & Supportive**: With subject lines, greeting placeholders, and warm body text.
- **Teacher Review Checklist**: A quick 4-point checklist the teacher must verify before hitting send (e.g., checking for bias, spelling, and calendar link details).
</output_format>

Please generate the parent-teacher email templates now.

## Variables
- {STUDENT_NAME} – The student's first name (e.g., Leo, Maya).
- {PARENT_NAME} – The parent's title/last name or first name if known (e.g., Mr. and Mrs. Smith, Ms. Johnson).
- {EMAIL_REASON} – The high-level concern (e.g., consistent disruptive talking during silent reading, sudden drop in algebra grades, sleeping in class, outstanding science project overdue).
- {SPECIFIC_DETAILS} – Exact facts/dates/metrics to provide context (e.g., grade dropped from a B to a D, put head down on desk 4 times this week, has missed the last two homework checks).
- {DESIRED_OUTCOME} – The goal of sending the email (e.g., scheduling a 10-minute phone call this week, establishing a weekly homework check sheet, enrolling the student in free Tuesday math tutoring).
- {TONE_PROFILE} – The desired communication tone (Options: "Warm, highly collaborative, and relational", "Firm, professional, and boundary-setting", "Empathetic, gentle, and developmental").

## Notes
- Building "parent alliances" early in the school year is one of the most effective classroom management strategies.
- Never write emails when angry; this prompt ensures a neutral, supportive tone that protects the teacher-parent relationship.
- If the reason is purely celebratory (e.g., sending a positive note home), the prompt will adjust to write a highly celebratory, encouraging "Good News" email that boosts student morale.
