## Purpose
Generates a comprehensive, professional, trauma-informed, and highly actionable Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) designed to identify behavioral functions, alter triggers, teach replacement behaviors, and outline de-escalation steps.

## Prompt
You are a Board Certified Behavior Analyst (BCBA), school psychologist, and specialist in multi-tiered systems of student support (MTSS). Your task is to draft a comprehensive, trauma-informed, and highly practical Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP) for a student exhibiting challenging behaviors in the classroom.

<student_profile>
- Grade Level/Age: {GRADE_LEVEL}
- Target/Challenging Behavior: {CHALLENGING_BEHAVIOR}
- Identified Triggers (Antecedents): {ANTECEDENTS}
- Current Teacher Reaction/Consequence: {CONSEQUENCES}
</student_profile>

<instructions>
1. **Define the Target Behavior & Hypothesize the Function (FBA)**:
   - Rewrite the challenging behavior in highly objective, observable, and measurable terms (avoiding subjective words like "defiant" or "lazy").
   - Analyze the antecedent triggers and current consequences to hypothesize the underlying **function** of the behavior (Options: Escape/Avoidance of task or social situation, Access to Attention from peers/teacher, Access to Tangibles/activities, or Sensory/automatic reinforcement). Explain your reasoning.

2. **Proactive (Antecedent) Strategies**:
   - Recommend 3 practical modifications to the classroom environment, instructional delivery, or schedule designed to minimize the triggers before the behavior starts (e.g., seating adjustments, visual supports, verbal pre-corrects, offering limited choices, task chunking/academic adaptations).

3. **Instruction of Functionally Equivalent Replacement Behavior (FERB)**:
   - Identify a positive, socially acceptable replacement behavior that serves the *exact same function* as the challenging behavior (e.g., teaching the student to use a "break card" rather than ripping up worksheets to escape a task).
   - Outline a clear, step-by-step instructional script showing how the teacher will explicitly teach and practice this replacement behavior during non-stressful moments.

4. **Reinforcement & Consequence (Reactive) Strategies**:
   - **Reinforcement Plan**: Propose a structured schedule of positive reinforcement to reward the student for using the replacement behavior (e.g., token economy, praise ratios, special privileges).
   - **De-escalation Protocol**: Provide a graduated sequence of low-emotion teacher responses when the student begins to escalate (Phase 1: Agitation, Phase 2: Acceleration, Phase 3: Peak, Phase 4: Recovery).
   - **Reactive/Consequence Strategy**: Outline how the teacher should respond when the target behavior is actively occurring. Ensure this response does *not* accidentally reinforce the behavior (e.g., avoiding giving attention if the function is attention-seeking).

5. **Data Collection & Progress Monitoring**:
   - Suggest a highly feasible, low-effort data-tracking method (e.g., daily behavior report card, interval tallies, post-it note counter) that the teacher can implement without disrupting instruction.
   - Establish a clear, measurable goal for success (e.g., "The target behavior will decrease to less than 1 episode per day, maintained over 3 consecutive weeks").
</instructions>

<output_format>
Structure the intervention plan into these sections:
- **I. Behavior Diagnostic & FBA Hypothesis**: Measurable definitions and behavioral functions.
- **II. Proactive Antecedent Strategies**: Actionable environmental and instructional adjustments.
- **III. Replacement Behavior Instruction (FERB)**: Detailed teaching steps and positive reinforcement protocols.
- **IV. Graduated Crisis/De-escalation & Consequence Protocol**: How to react to escalating behaviors.
- **V. Progress Monitoring & Data Collection Plan**: Data tracking tools and quantitative success criteria.
- **VI. Teacher Quick-Card**: A high-level, 1-page summary checklist of do's and don'ts for daily classroom reference.
</output_format>

Please generate the Behavior Intervention Plan now.

## Variables
- {GRADE_LEVEL} – The student's age or grade level (e.g., 2nd Grade, 8th Grade, High School Sophomore).
- {CHALLENGING_BEHAVIOR} – The specific, problematic behavior observed in the classroom (e.g., shouting out, refusing to start work, leaving their seat constantly, physical agitation/slamming books).
- {ANTECEDENTS} – What typically happens immediately before the behavior occurs (e.g., during independent writing tasks, when transition announcements are made, when redirected by a female teacher, when working in large groups).
- {CONSEQUENCES} – What happens immediately after the behavior occurs (e.g., teacher redirects verbally, peers laugh, student is sent to the hallway/office, student sits quietly doing nothing).

## Notes
- A BIP is a living document. It should be reviewed every 4 to 6 weeks to evaluate data trends and adjust strategies.
- Ensure the tone remains entirely objective, professional, and centered on supporting the student's needs, avoiding blaming or punitive language.
- Highly useful for general education teachers collaborating with Special Education (IEP/504) teams.
