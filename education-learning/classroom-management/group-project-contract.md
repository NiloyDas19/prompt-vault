## Purpose
Creates a highly structured, student-facing Group Project Alignment Contract that establishes clear roles, milestone deadlines, communication rules, conflict resolution steps, and a fair peer-intervention/firing protocol.

## Prompt
You are an expert instructional designer and project-based learning (PBL) facilitator. Your goal is to draft a comprehensive, student-facing "Group Project Alignment Contract & Accountability Guide" that students will complete and sign at the beginning of a collaborative group project to prevent slacking and manage team dynamics.

<context>
- Grade Level/Academic Stage: {GRADE_LEVEL}
- Subject/Class: {SUBJECT}
- Project Topic/Scope: {PROJECT_SCOPE}
- Group Size: {GROUP_SIZE}
</context>

<instructions>
1. **Draft in Student-Friendly Language**:
   - Write the entire contract in an empowering, professional, yet easy-to-understand tone suitable for {GRADE_LEVEL}. Make it feel like a professional workplace charter.

2. **Core Contract Components**:
   - **Team Mission & Grade Goals**: Guided questions for the team to define their collective standards, work ethic, and goal grade.
   - **Role Assignments & Responsibilities Matrix**: A set of standardized group roles adapted for {GROUP_SIZE} (e.g., Project Manager/Facilitator, Lead Researcher, Creative/Visual Director, Editor/Quality Control) with clear, measurable responsibilities for each role.
   - **Communication & Response Agreements**: Structured fill-in sections defining communication channels, meeting frequencies, and expected response times (e.g., "We will respond to group messages within 24 hours").
   - **Milestone & Individual Task Tracker**: A clean markdown table where students must list specific deliverables, individual deadlines, and the team member responsible for each piece.

3. **Conflict Resolution & Firing Protocol**:
   - Create a 3-step, restorative conflict-resolution pathway for teams to resolve internal friction before bringing the issue to the teacher (e.g., Step 1: Direct, polite peer confrontation; Step 2: Team meeting to revise task tracker; Step 3: Request teacher mediation).
   - Outline a clear, formal "Peer Firing Protocol" describing the grounds, warnings, and consequences if a group member fails to meet their obligations. If "fired" from a group, detail the alternative individual work they will be required to submit to avoid failing.

4. **Self & Peer Evaluation Rubric**:
   - Design a highly practical rubrics table (out of 5 points) measuring: Contribution, Reliability/Punctuality, Quality of Work, and Collaboration. Include a section for qualitative feedback.
</instructions>

<output_format>
Draft the documents under these distinct headings:
- **I. Team Charter & Project Contract**: The fillable student agreement.
- **II. Role Descriptions & Task Tracker Table**: Detailed descriptions of roles and empty milestone charts.
- **III. Team Conflict Resolution & Firing Protocol**: The step-by-step rules of accountability.
- **IV. Mid-Point & Final Peer Evaluation Rubric**: Student evaluation sheets.
- **V. Teacher Facilitation Notes**: Quick tips on how to introduce the contract, check in on student progress, and use peer evaluations to adjust individual grades.
</output_format>

Please generate the group project contract package now.

## Variables
- {GRADE_LEVEL} – The student age range (e.g., 6th Grade, High School Juniors, College Freshmen, MBA Candidates).
- {SUBJECT} – The subject area (e.g., Environmental Science, World History, Business Marketing, Engineering).
- {PROJECT_SCOPE} – The high-level description of what students are creating (e.g., A collaborative research paper and 10-minute presentation, building a working physics model, writing a business plan).
- {GROUP_SIZE} – The average size of a project group (e.g., 3 members, 4 members, 5 members).

## Notes
- Handing students a group project without a team contract often leads to unequal workloads and intense student friction. This contract builds critical workplace collaboration skills.
- Have students sign the contract and submit it on Day 1 of the project, then use it as a reference tool if conflicts arise.
- If a student is fired, they must complete the entire scope of the project alone or receive a zero, which strongly deters social loafing.
