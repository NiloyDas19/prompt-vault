## Purpose
Drafts deep, open-ended classroom discussion prompts calibrated to Bloom's Taxonomy, paired with scaffolding Socratic follow-up questions and custom facilitation protocols to maximize student engagement and equity.

## Prompt
You are an expert instructional designer and experienced Socratic seminar facilitator. Your task is to draft a series of high-quality, open-ended classroom discussion prompts designed to stimulate deep critical thinking, collaborative analysis, and inclusive participation.

<context>
- Grade Level/Age Group: {GRADE_LEVEL}
- Subject/Topic: {DISCUSSION_TOPIC}
- Core Learning Objective: {LEARNING_OBJECTIVE}
- Chosen Discussion Format: {DISCUSSION_FORMAT}
- Number of Prompts: {PROMPT_COUNT}
</context>

<instructions>
1. **Formulate High-Cognitive Core Prompts**:
   - Generate {PROMPT_COUNT} open-ended discussion prompts. Avoid binary (yes/no) questions or queries with simple factual answers.
   - Frame them as ethical dilemmas, real-world paradoxes, competing philosophies, or analytical comparisons that align with the higher levels of Bloom's Taxonomy (Analyzing, Evaluating, Creating).
   - For each prompt, state the target cognitive skill it exercises and why it is effective for {GRADE_LEVEL}.

2. **Develop Socratic Scaffolding & Follow-Ups**:
   - For *each* core prompt, provide 2 or 3 Socratic follow-up questions the teacher can use to deepen the discourse when student answers are superficial. Examples:
     - Clarification prompts (e.g., "What do you mean when you use the word...?")
     - Probing assumptions (e.g., "What are we taking for granted in this scenario?")
     - Alternative perspectives (e.g., "How would an advocate for [opposing view] respond to that argument?")
     - Consequence analysis (e.g., "If we accept that premise, what does that imply for...?")

3. **Create the Facilitation Protocol**:
   - Provide a step-by-step guide explaining how to set up and run the selected {DISCUSSION_FORMAT}.
   - Detail the structural stages: Pre-discussion preparation, the active discussion phase, and the synthesis/wrap-up phase.

4. **Equity & Classroom Management Strategies**:
   - Provide 3 tailored tips for managing room dynamics in this specific format:
     - How to lower the barrier to entry for quiet, anxious, or introverted students.
     - How to diplomatically redirect a student who is dominating the verbal airtime.
     - A set of 4 student-friendly "Discussion Norms" or ground rules.
</instructions>

<output_format>
Structure your response as follows:
- **Topic Framing & Hook**: A brief, 3-sentence script the teacher can use to introduce the topic and set the mood.
- **The Discussion Prompt Matrix**: A list of the core prompts, cognitive targets, and Socratic follow-ups.
- **Discussion Protocol Blueprint**: Actionable directions for executing {DISCUSSION_FORMAT}.
- **Inclusive Dialogue Toolkit**: Classroom management tips for maintaining voice equity and behavioral flow.
</output_format>

Please construct the discussion prompts and facilitation guide now.

## Variables
- {GRADE_LEVEL} – The student age range or grade (e.g., 5th Grade, Middle School History, High School Literature, College Freshman Philosophy, Adult Seminar).
- {DISCUSSION_TOPIC} – The subject matter, text, or historical event (e.g., The decision to drop the atomic bomb, Lord of the Flies Chapter 3, Ethical implications of artificial intelligence in art, The water cycle and climate change).
- {LEARNING_OBJECTIVE} – The desired cognitive outcome (e.g., Evaluate moral responsibility, synthesize multiple sources of evidence, analyze systemic causes).
- {DISCUSSION_FORMAT} – The pedagogical discussion structure (Options: "Think-Pair-Share", "Inner-Outer Circle/Fishbowl", "Socratic Seminar", "Philosophical Chairs", "Silent Chalk Talk/Gallery Walk", "Speed Dating Discussion").
- {PROMPT_COUNT} – Number of core prompts to generate (typically 3 to 5).

## Notes
- Discussion works best when teachers step back from the "sage on the stage" role and act as "guides on the side," allowing students to directly address one another.
- Socratic prompts help students learn *how* to think rather than *what* to think, building academic resilience.
- For remote classrooms, "Silent Chalk Talk" can be conducted using online collaborative boards (e.g., Padlet or Miro).
