## Purpose
Compiles positive, student-friendly classroom expectations, a context-specific behavioral matrix (PBIS style), a restorative consequence pathway, and a first-week co-creation lesson plan to establish a strong classroom culture.

## Prompt
You are an expert in Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports (PBIS), restorative justice in education, and classroom management systems. Your goal is to draft a comprehensive, highly positive, and practical "Classroom Expectations & Rules Blueprint" tailored to a specific classroom profile.

<context>
- Grade Level/Age Group: {GRADE_LEVEL}
- Subject/Class Type: {SUBJECT}
- Core Classroom Values: {CORE_VALUES}
- Vibe/Culture Goal: {CULTURE_GOAL}
</context>

<instructions>
1. **Translate Rules into Positive Commitments**:
   - Take 4 typical negative classroom rules (e.g., "No talking while the teacher talks", "No phones", "Don't pack up early", "No copying work") and rewrite them as positive, active behavioral commitments that align with {CORE_VALUES} (e.g., "Active Listening: We respect our peers and teacher by giving them our full attention when they share").

2. **Draft a Context-Specific Behavioral Matrix**:
   - Construct a structured PBIS-style markdown table outlining exactly what the {CORE_VALUES} look and sound like in different school settings.
   - Columns: [Core Value | Whole-Group Instruction | Independent Focus Time | Small-Group Collaboration | Transitions & Clean-Up].
   - Write these behaviors in clear, observable action phrases suitable for {GRADE_LEVEL}.

3. **Establish a Restorative Consequence Pathway**:
   - Design a graduated, restorative hierarchy of consequences that teachers can follow consistently.
   - Ensure the pathway prioritizes self-regulation, restoration of the community, and teacher-student collaboration before escalating to office discipline referrals. Include:
     - **Tier 1 (Proactive)**: Non-verbal cues, proximity control, and verbal redirections.
     - **Tier 2 (Reflective)**: A private, respectful teacher-student check-in and temporary seat adjustment.
     - **Tier 3 (Restorative)**: A student self-reflection form (provide 3 prompt questions) and parent contact to form a joint support plan.
     - **Tier 4 (Administrative)**: Formal referral and collaborative recovery meeting.

4. **Day 1-3 Co-Creation and Practice Lesson Plan**:
   - Create a highly interactive, 3-day mini-lesson outline for the first week of school to teach, practice, and get student buy-in on these guidelines:
     - **Day 1: Co-creating the classroom contract** (How students help define these values).
     - **Day 2: "Looks Like / Sounds Like" modeling** (Students act out correct vs. incorrect examples).
     - **Day 3: Interactive practice and sign-off** (Confirming agreements).
</instructions>

<output_format>
Present your compiled blueprint under these markdown headings:
- **I. Classroom Vision & Positive Commitments**: The rewritten, values-aligned classroom expectations.
- **II. The Positive Behavioral Matrix**: The markdown table showing values in action.
- **III. Restorative Consequence Flowchart**: Step-by-step hierarchy of interventions.
- **IV. Student Self-Reflection Template**: Questions for the student reflection sheet.
- **V. First-Week Rollout Lesson Plan**: The 3-day lesson guide.
</output_format>

Please generate the expectations and rules blueprint now.

## Variables
- {GRADE_LEVEL} – The student age range (e.g., Kindergarten, 5th Grade, 8th Grade Middle School, 11th Grade High School, College Lecture).
- {SUBJECT} – The subject area (e.g., Chemistry Lab, English Language Arts, Mathematics, Art/Studio, General Homeroom).
- {CORE_VALUES} – 3 or 4 foundational adjectives that define the class (e.g., Respect, Accountability, Courage, Empathy, Growth).
- {CULTURE_GOAL} – The desired behavioral environment (e.g., "A highly collaborative, loud, and creative design studio vibe", "A quiet, highly focused, organized, and structured learning environment", "An active, hands-on scientific inquiry space where safety is paramount").

## Notes
- Post the Positive Behavioral Matrix prominently in the classroom (or pin it to the virtual classroom homepage) so you can point to it when redirecting behavior.
- Co-creating the rules with students on Day 1 drastically increases compliance, as students feel they are enforcing *their* rules, not the *teacher's* rules.
- Consistently refer to "choices" and "agreements" rather than "rules" and "punishments" to build student agency and executive functioning.
