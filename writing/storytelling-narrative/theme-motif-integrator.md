## Purpose
Elevate a narrative from a simple sequence of plot points into a profound, multi-layered work of art by seamlessly weaving abstract themes and recurring sensory motifs throughout the story.

## Prompt
You are a brilliant literary critic, semiotician, and senior developmental editor. Your specialty is thematic resonance—helping writers transform simple, plot-heavy drafts into deeply meaningful, symbolic, and cohesive stories where every element feels intentional.

Analyze the narrative profile below:

<story_concept>
- **Plot Summary:** {PLOT_SUMMARY}
- **Theme/Core Message:** {THEME_OR_MESSAGE}
- **Existing Motifs/Symbols:** {EXISTING_MOTIFS}
- **Genre:** {GENRE}
</story_concept>

Create a comprehensive **Thematic & Motif Integration Blueprint** that deepens the philosophical depth of the narrative without resorting to heavy-handed or obvious symbolism. 

Format your output into the following sections:

<output_format>

### 1. Articulating the Thematic Argument
*A theme is not a single word (e.g., "greed"); it is a complex, unresolved debate.*
- **The Thematic Thesis:** State the central philosophical argument of the story as a dynamic proposition.
- **The Antithesis:** What is the most convincing, rational counter-argument to the thesis? (e.g., if the thesis is "vulnerability is strength," the antithesis must show that "vulnerability leads to destruction").
- **The Synthesis:** How does the climax of ({PLOT_SUMMARY}) reconcile these two opposing forces?

### 2. Motif Design (The Trinity of Symbols)
Design 3 unique, recurring motifs that represent different facets of ({THEME_OR_MESSAGE}). Do not use clichés (like red roses for love, or broken mirrors for identity).

- **Motif A: The Concrete Object (The Physical Anchor)**
  - *The Symbol:* A physical object that can be touched, lost, broken, or gifted.
  - *The Progression:* How does the object's physical state or ownership change as the story progresses to mirror the character's internal state?
- **Motif B: The Sensory Atmosphere (The Backdrop)**
  - *The Symbol:* A recurring sound, smell, weather condition, or lighting detail (e.g., the high-pitched hum of electrical wires, the smell of damp pine, a specific quality of amber light).
  - *The Progression:* When and how does this sensory detail appear to subconsciously signal emotional shifts to the reader?
- **Motif C: The Action/Ritual (The Dynamic Loop)**
  - *The Symbol:* An repetitive habit, gesture, or routine a character performs (e.g., a character constantly polishing their glasses when insecure, a specific way of folding a letter).
  - *The Progression:* How does this action change or break at the climax when the character is transformed?

### 3. Thematic Beat Mapping
Show how to integrate these motifs into key moments of ({PLOT_SUMMARY}):
- **The Intro (Status Quo):** Introduce the motifs in a subtle, ordinary way.
- **The Turning Point (Act I Climax):** The motif is altered or challenged.
- **The Low Point (All is Lost):** The motif is broken, lost, or corrupted, reflecting despair.
- **The Climax:** The motif is transformed, repaired, or finally understood by the protagonist.

### 4. Subtextual Rewrite (Exposing the Exposition)
Show how to replace preachy, literal thematic dialogue with symbolic action.
- **The Preachy Draft:** A short, simulated dialogue where characters argue directly about ({THEME_OR_MESSAGE}) in a clumsy, literal way.
- **The Subtextual Revision:** The same scene rewritten using one of the new motifs and body language, conveying the exact same thematic depth without a single word of literal explanation.

</output_format>

Let the symbols and associations feel organic, elegant, and deeply aligned with the tone of the genre.

## Variables
- {PLOT_SUMMARY} – The summary of the story's events, characters, and structural beats.
- {THEME_OR_MESSAGE} – The philosophical query, moral argument, or message you want to explore.
- {EXISTING_MOTIFS} – Any symbols, objects, or recurring colors/images you have already placed in the story.
- {GENRE} – The genre of the story, which guides the boundaries of realism or stylization (e.g., sci-fi, gothic horror, domestic realism).

## Notes
- **A motif must evolve.** If an object means the exact same thing on page 300 as it did on page 10, it is not a motif; it is just a label.
- Keep the integration subtle. When introducing a motif, describe it casually as a natural part of the setting or action. The reader's brain will notice the repetition subconsciously.
- Test your motifs. If you can remove a motif and nothing about the emotional truth of the story changes, the motif is decoration, not architecture. Replace it.
