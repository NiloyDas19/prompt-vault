## Purpose
Design and braid compelling secondary narrative threads (subplots) that enrich the main plot, flesh out secondary characters, and amplify the story's core themes without slowing down pacing.

## Prompt
You are a master dramaturg, bestselling novelist, and narrative structuralist. Your expertise is in plot architecture—specifically, the art of weaving complex, engaging subplots (B and C stories) that beautifully mirror, intersect, and resolve alongside the main plot (A story).

Review the primary story architecture below:

<story_architecture>
- **Main Plot (A-Story):** {MAIN_PLOT}
- **Primary Characters:** {PRIMARY_CHARACTERS}
- **Secondary Characters:** {SECONDARY_CHARACTERS}
- **Thematic Focus:** {THEMATIC_FOCUS}
- **Genre:** {GENRE}
</story_architecture>

Create a robust **Subplot Integration Blueprint** that constructs two distinct, high-impact subplots. These subplots must not feel like "filler" content; they must either directly assist, actively complicate, or symbolically reflect the main quest.

Format your output into the following sections:

<output_format>

### 1. Subplot Conception
Design two separate subplots using the following structural archetypes:

- **Subplot B: The Emotional Mirror (Relational Thread)**
  - *The Concept:* A relationship-driven thread (romance, mentorship, friendship, family rivalry) involving ({SECONDARY_CHARACTERS}) that forces the protagonist to confront their inner wound.
  - *The Mirror Effect:* How does this relationship reflect ({THEMATIC_FOCUS}) in a way the main plot cannot?
- **Subplot C: The Logistical Friction (The Practical Obstacle)**
  - *The Concept:* A lower-stakes, highly concrete problem or mystery that must be resolved (e.g., hiding a secret, passing a test, procuring a specific resource, a minor betrayal).
  - *The Intersect:* How does this logistical struggle directly interfere with the progression of ({MAIN_PLOT})?

### 2. The Braiding Grid (Structural Beats)
Show how to alternate and weave these threads through the narrative acts. Provide a beat-by-beat outline of how the scenes interlace.

- **Act I: Introduction & Inciting Action**
  - *Beat A1 (Main Plot):* The main quest begins.
  - *Beat B1 (Mirror Subplot):* Introduce the relationship and its inherent tension or promise.
  - *Beat C1 (Friction Subplot):* The minor logistical problem appears.
- **Act II: Rising Complication & The Tangle**
  - *Beat A2 (Main Plot):* Main obstacles escalate.
  - *Beat C2 (Friction Subplot):* The minor problem worsens, creating a sudden roadblock for the A-story.
  - *Beat B2 (Mirror Subplot):* The relationship deepens, but a hidden secret or vulnerability threatens to break it.
  - *The Midpoint Collision:* Map a scene where all three threads clash. (e.g., during a critical main-plot confrontation, the minor subplot problem erupts, and a relationship secret is exposed).
- **Act III: The Low Point & Resolution**
  - *Beat B3 (Mirror Subplot Resolution):* The relationship is either saved or tragically lost, giving the protagonist the key emotional breakthrough they need.
  - *Beat C3 (Friction Subplot Resolution):* The logistical problem is finally solved, unlocking a resource or truth.
  - *Beat A3 (Main Plot Climax):* The protagonist uses the emotional breakthrough (from B) and the resource/truth (from C) to resolve the primary conflict of ({MAIN_PLOT}).

### 3. Secondary Character Arcs
For the key secondary characters in ({SECONDARY_CHARACTERS}):
- **Their Personal Quest:** What is their independent goal in their subplot?
- **Their Impact on the Hero:** How do they force the protagonist to change?

### 4. Pacing & Transition Techniques
- **The "Baton Pass":** Show two examples of scene transitions where the end of a main plot scene naturally launches a subplot scene (e.g., using a visual match cut or a unresolved question).

</output_format>

Ensure that both subplots feel essential, emotional, and logically integrated into the primary story.

## Variables
- {MAIN_PLOT} – The primary plotline, goal, and physical journey of the protagonist (the A-Story).
- {PRIMARY_CHARACTERS} – The main protagonist, their fatal flaw, and the main antagonist/rival.
- {SECONDARY_CHARACTERS} – The side characters, mentors, love interests, or comedic reliefs who will lead the subplots.
- {THEMATIC_FOCUS} – The central thematic debate or message of the work.
- {GENRE} – The structural genre of the narrative (e.g., mystery, cozy fantasy, high-octane space opera).

## Notes
- **A subplot is a counterweight.** If the A-story is packed with physical action, use the B-story to slow down and explore psychological or emotional intimacy.
- Avoid the "disconnected episodic" feel. Every scene in a subplot must still drive forward—never allow a subplot scene to merely recap things the reader already knows.
- Use the B and C stories to give the main character temporary victories when the A-story is at its darkest, or vice-versa, keeping the emotional pacing dynamic.
