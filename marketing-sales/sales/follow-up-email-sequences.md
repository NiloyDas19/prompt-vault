## Purpose
Write strategic follow-up emails for different sales scenarios that keep deals alive without being annoying.

## Prompt
Write follow-up email sequences for these common sales situations. Each email should add new value — never just "checking in."

**My Company:** {COMPANY}
**My Product:** {PRODUCT}
**My Role:** {MY_ROLE}
**Brand Voice:** {TONE}

### Scenario 1: After a Discovery Call (3 emails)
**Email 1 (Same day):** Recap the call, confirm next steps, attach relevant resource
**Email 2 (Day 3):** Share a relevant case study or resource that wasn't discussed
**Email 3 (Day 7):** If no response, gentle nudge with a new angle or insight

### Scenario 2: After Sending a Proposal (3 emails)
**Email 1 (Day 2):** "Want to walk through the proposal together?"
**Email 2 (Day 5):** Address a common concern proactively
**Email 3 (Day 10):** Final follow-up with a modified offer or extended deadline

### Scenario 3: After Being Ghosted (3 emails)
**Email 1 (Day 14):** Provide value without asking for anything
**Email 2 (Day 21):** "Quick question" — ask something easy to reply to
**Email 3 (Day 30):** "Should I close your file?" — the breakup email

### Scenario 4: After a "Not Now" Response (2 emails)
**Email 1 (Immediately):** Acknowledge, ask when to check back, set a reminder
**Email 2 (At the agreed time):** Re-engage with something new that's changed since you last spoke

For each email:
- **Subject line** (max 40 characters)
- **Body** (under 75 words — short is king)
- **CTA** (one clear ask)
- **Value added** — what new thing are you bringing to the conversation?
- **Psychological principle** at work

## Variables
- {COMPANY} – Your company name
- {PRODUCT} – What you sell
- {MY_ROLE} – Your title
- {TONE} – Voice (e.g., "friendly and casual", "professional and direct")

## Notes
- Rule: NEVER send a follow-up that just says "just checking in" or "circling back." Every follow-up must deliver new value.
- 80% of sales require 5+ follow-ups, but 44% of salespeople give up after 1. Persistence wins.
- The "breakup email" ("Should I close your file?") has a 30%+ reply rate — it triggers loss aversion.
